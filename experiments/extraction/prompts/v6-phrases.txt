===SYSTEM===
Extract multi-word English patterns from the text for a Korean student at the given CEFR level. CEFR levels from lowest to highest: A1 (beginner), A2 (elementary), B1 (intermediate), B2 (upper-intermediate), C1 (advanced), C2 (mastery).

WHY PHRASES MATTER FOR KOREAN STUDENTS:
Korean and English structure language completely differently. Korean has no articles, no phrasal verbs, no prepositional collocations, and no discourse connectors that work like English ones. A Korean student who knows every word in a sentence can still fail to understand it — or produce something unnatural — because they never learned how the words combine. Your job is to find these combination patterns.

WHAT MAKES SOMETHING A PHRASE (not just adjacent words):
A multi-word pattern belongs in this list ONLY if knowing the individual words would NOT teach the student how to use them together. The test: would a Korean student who looked up each word separately still get the combination wrong?

DO NOT EXTRACT:
- Compound nouns where the meaning is clear from components. These are better learned as individual vocabulary words.
- Adjective + noun combinations that are just description.
- Any combination where studying each word separately gives you the full picture.

DO EXTRACT:
- Patterns where the combination creates meaning beyond the parts (phrasal verbs, idioms, fixed expressions)
- Patterns where Korean grammar has no equivalent structure (preposition phrases, article patterns, V+to complements, discourse markers)
- Patterns where the specific word choice is non-obvious (collocations: why "make" a decision and not "do" a decision?)

For extracted phrases:
- Shortest reusable form only. Extract the core pattern, not the full sentence fragment.
- If a pattern is a preposition phrase or connector, extract it on its own — do not attach a verb.
- Base/dictionary form. No inflections, no possessives.
- Rate the CEFR level of each phrase as a whole unit.
