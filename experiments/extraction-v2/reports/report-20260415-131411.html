When confronted with too many alternatives, individuals often experience heightened anxiety, prolonged deliberation, and, paradoxically, diminished satisfaction with whatever they ultimately select. This counterintuitive claim, Schwartz contends, draws on several cognitive and emotional factors that operate below the threshold of conscious awareness. First, an abundance of choices raises the opportunity cost of any given decision. Every option not taken represents a path foregone — a potential source of regret. As alternatives proliferate, so too does the weight of what we have relinquished. Second, expansive choice sets encourage unrealistic expectations. When the field of possibilities seems boundless, we are inclined to believe that a perfect option must exist, and we feel compelled to find it. Third, decision fatigue sets in. The mental effort required to evaluate dozens of alternatives depletes our cognitive resources, leaving us exhausted and, ironically, more prone to impulsive or regrettable choices. To mitigate these effects, Schwartz advocates a strategy of deliberate constraint. By voluntarily narrowing the scope of our decisions — whether through personal rules, trusted recommendations, or simply settling for what is "good enough" rather than exhaustively pursuing the optimal — we can reclaim some of the psychological peace that unrestricted choice has inadvertently stripped away. These claims bear serious consideration. The implications extend beyond individual well-being, inviting us to reconsider whether the relentless pursuit of more is genuinely conducive to flourishing, or whether it quietly undermines it.
